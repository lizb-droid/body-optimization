## Weekly Series Strategy

The Wednesday series races are a gift — weekly race-day practice with low stakes. Use them intentionally:

- **Weeks 1–3:** Run by effort, not pace. Get comfortable with the course format. Practice your fueling protocol.
- **Weeks 4–6:** Start pushing. Use these as threshold efforts — race the uphills hard, practice downhill mechanics.
- **Weeks 7+:** Full race effort. These are fitness tests. Note how you feel relative to cycle phase each week.
- **The week before each Cirque race:** Treat the Wednesday series race as an easy shakeout — don't race it hard.

**Note on recovery:** Racing every Wednesday means Wednesday is no longer a "run day" on your training schedule — it's a race. Manage Tuesday accordingly (easy or rest, not intervals). Saturday trail run continues as planned but may be easier effort in summer peak weeks.

## Trail-Specific Training Elements

### 1. Uphill Running

The biggest differentiator in trail races is uphill efficiency. Two approaches:

- **Running uphills:** Shorten stride, increase cadence, lean slightly forward from ankles (not waist), drive arms
- **Power hiking uphills:** For steep grades (>15%) or late in a race — this is a strategy, not failure. Many elite trail runners power hike steep sections and run everything else.

**Training application:**
- Hill repeats: 6–10 × 60–90 seconds hard uphill, walk back down
- Long run: Seek elevation. Walk all grades >12–15% at first, build to running them.

### 2. Downhill Running

Technically demanding. Eccentric quad loading. Injury risk if undertrained.

**Key mechanics:**
- Lean slightly forward (trust the hill)
- Shorten stride, higher cadence
- Look ahead (not at feet) — 10–15 ft down trail
- Soft knees — never lock out on impact

**Training application:**
- After hill repeats, practice controlled downhill running
- Bulgarian split squats + single-leg RDLs build the eccentric quad strength needed
- Don't ignore this. Trashed quads on descents are the #1 complaint of undertrained trail runners.

### 3. Technical Terrain

For the 3,000 ft technical races:
- Practice on uneven surfaces — grass, gravel, varied terrain
- Single-leg stability work in the gym (Bulgarian split squat, step-ups, lateral lunges) translates directly
- Slow down on technical sections — better to be conservative than to roll an ankle
- Look further ahead than feels natural

## Race-Day Fueling Protocol (Practice in Training First)

### Dr. Sims on Women & Race Fueling

Women oxidize fat at higher rates than men during exercise. But this does NOT mean you should skip carbs during races. At higher intensities (which trail races often demand), you need glycogen.

**Key Sims point:** Women respond better to lower-fructose carb sources during racing (vs. high-fructose gels that many male-focused products use). Look for glucose-based or maltodextrin-based products.

### Race Fueling by Race Type

**Weekly Series — 4–6 miles, ~1,500 ft (~50–80 min)**
- Pre-race: Normal pre-workout meal — 20–25g protein + 20–30g carbs, 60–90 min before start
- During: Water + electrolytes. If over 60 min, take in 1–2 dates, half a gel, or applesauce pouch around mile 2–3.
- Post-race: Within 30 min — 25–30g protein + 30–40g carbs. You still have the rest of your day.
- Wednesday timing note: These are likely morning or evening races. Adjust pre-race meal timing accordingly. If evening — eat normally all day, have a small carb + protein snack 60–90 min before.

**Cirque Series — ~8 miles, ~3,000 ft technical (~90–150 min)**
- Pre-race: Full meal 2–2.5 hours before. Oats + protein powder, or eggs + toast + banana. No high fat or fiber.
- During: Start fueling at 35–40 min — don't wait until you feel it. 30–45g carbs/hour. Real food works well: dates, banana pieces, applesauce pouches, Honey Stinger waffles. Low-fructose options preferred.
- Hydration: 16–20 oz/hour minimum. Electrolytes from the start — don't wait.
- Post-race: Full recovery meal within 45 min. This is a hard effort — treat recovery nutrition seriously. Milk supply may dip — eat and hydrate aggressively for 24 hours after.
- Practice: Run your pre-Cirque Saturday long runs with the exact fueling you'll use race day. No experiments on race day.

## Race Week Protocols

### 7 Days Out
- Reduce run volume 30–40% (shorter runs, same intensity)
- Keep lifting but reduce volume by 30%
- Sleep is the priority — aim for 8+ hours
- Hydration: Begin building fluid intake, add electrolytes

### 3–4 Days Out
- Easy movement only — 20–30 min jog, some strides to stay sharp
- Carb loading begins mildly (not extreme — just don't restrict carbs)
- Start eating iron-rich foods if period is near (timing can affect race performance)
- Lay out gear, check shoes, prepare nutrition

### Night Before
- Normal dinner, slightly higher carbs (pasta, rice, sweet potato)
- Protein: normal amount
- Avoid high-fiber foods, too much fat, anything new
- Bed early. Even if you can't sleep, rest.

### Race Morning
- Wake up 2–2.5 hours before race start if possible
- Eat: Familiar meal — eggs + toast, oatmeal + protein powder, banana + nut butter
- Avoid dairy the morning of if prone to GI issues
- Coffee is fine if you drink it normally (don't experiment with caffeine if you don't)
- Breastfeeding: Feed or pump before the race if timing allows — comfort and supply

## Elevation Training on Limited Time

You have 60 minutes. Here's how to get elevation-specific fitness:
- Treadmill incline runs: Set to 8–12% incline, walk/run 20–30 min
- Stadium stairs: If accessible — incredible uphill specificity
- Hill repeats on nearby hill: More valuable than flat running for race prep

**Strength gym work that transfers:**
- Hip thrust (glute power = uphill engine)
- Step-up with high knee (trail mechanics)
- Single-leg RDL (downhill stability)
- Plyometrics (power, foot turnover)

## Cirque Series — Technical Race Specific Prep

The Cirque races are a different animal than the weekly series. 8 miles + 3,000 ft of technical terrain demands specific preparation beyond general fitness.

### What Makes Cirque Different
- **Technical footing** — loose rock, narrow trail, uneven surfaces. Ankle stability and foot placement become as important as fitness.
- **3,000 ft of gain** — expect long sustained climbs. Power hiking sections is a strategy, not failure. Practice it.
- **3,000 ft of descent** — this is where races are won or lost and where injuries happen. Eccentric quad strength is non-negotiable.
- **Time on feet** — at 90–150+ min, this is a fueling race. Going out too hard on the climb and bonking on the descent is the most common mistake.

### Cirque-Specific Training Add-Ons (Starting 6 Weeks Out)
- **Saturday long runs:** Start seeking the most technical, steep terrain available. Practice technical downhill at controlled speed.
- **Treadmill incline intervals:** 10–15% grade, 6–8 × 3 min hard, 2 min easy. Simulates sustained Cirque climbing.
- **Gym:** Prioritize Bulgarian split squat, single-leg RDL, and step-downs (eccentric focused) in the 6 weeks before each Cirque race.
- **Mental rehearsal:** Walk the course or study the elevation profile. Know where the big climb is. Know where you'll fuel. Remove decisions on race day.

### Cirque Race Week
- **Sunday–Monday before:** Normal training, slightly reduced volume
- **Tuesday:** Easy 20–30 min jog + strides. Legs should feel fresh.
- **Wednesday series race:** Easy effort only — do not race it. Use it as a warmup for the week.
- **Thursday:** Rest or short walk
- **Friday:** 15–20 min very easy shakeout, a few uphill strides
- **Saturday:** Race day

## Postpartum + Breastfeeding Race Considerations

1. **Race-day hydration is extra critical.** You're producing milk and racing. Electrolytes from the start.
2. **Feed or pump before the race.** Being engorged mid-race is both uncomfortable and a supply management issue.
3. **Sports bra fit matters more right now.** Nursing breasts are larger and more sensitive. Test your race-day bra on long runs beforehand.
4. **Bonking (hypoglycemia) hits differently while breastfeeding.** Your blood glucose can drop faster. Fuel earlier than you think you need to.
5. **Recovery post-race:** Milk supply may dip temporarily after a very hard race effort. Eat and hydrate aggressively in the 24 hours after.
6. **Be proud.** You are racing 9+ months postpartum while breastfeeding. That is not small.

## Race Goals Framework

Rather than time goals (which are impossible to predict with new trails + postpartum return), set process goals:

- **Race A goal (minimum success):** Finish strong, execute nutrition plan, run all runnable sections
- **Race B goal (reach):** Negative split the second half, maintain form on descents
- **Race C goal (dream):** Podium age group, PR a course

Track how you feel and what you executed — not just your place.
